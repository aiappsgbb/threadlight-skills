Threadlight  /  Production-ready

Three postures · thirteen pillars · one scorecard

Green safe-check isn’t go-live.

A pilot that passes threadlight-safe-check --phase post-deploy is structurally complete: every selector landed, every channel reaches, no placeholder image. That is not the same as ready for the customer architecture review. This chapter is what we walk through with the customer to answer “what posture is this in, what’s missing, who owns each gap, can we go live with waivers?”

threadlight-production-ready produces the answer in one command — soft-advisory (never fails a build), graceful when Azure permissions are partial (degrades to not-verified, never invents a green), and posture-aware (it doesn’t recommend Citadel if the customer is on a brownfield gateway).

When to graduate

Five questions a CISO asks. If any one fires, you’ve outgrown the laptop.

Threadlight skills run cleanly from a developer’s machine for early-stage co-design. The moment any of these five triggers turns red, the pilot needs a hosted runtime, governed identity, and a paved-path posture — not another iteration on the laptop. This is the conversation production-ready opens with.

01 · Identity

Who is the agent acting as?

If you can’t answer in one sentence — UAMI, Entra app, on-behalf-of — you’re past laptop scope.

02 · Audit

What did it do, when, for whom?

Append-only audit and OTel traces are the difference between a demo and a defensible deployment.

03 · RBAC

Can it read this index / container / API?

Keyless RBAC per service, scoped to the agent identity — not a developer’s personal token.

04 · Telemetry

How do we know it’s running well?

App Insights + OTel from day one, not bolted on after the first incident.

05 · Evals

How do we know the next deploy didn’t regress?

Foundry Evals as a continuous gate — quality is a release criterion, not a vibe-check.

Postures

Three shapes — resolved from SPEC § 12.

The right answer for “is this production-ready?” depends on the customer’s estate. We support three postures explicitly, and the safe-check skill resolves which one applies before it starts asking governance questions.

01 · Citadel spokeDEFAULT

Perimeter plus core.

Customer’s AI Citadel hub fronts every model call via APIM (JWT, rate-limit, content filter, semantic cache, ledger). Pilot Foundry project is onboarded as a spoke; agent-governance-toolkit enforces fine-grained policy inside the agent itself.

02 · AGT-only, in-process

Core only.

No central AI gateway. Agent Governance Toolkit (v4.1 preview, detected automatically) sits inside the agent process. 8–12 µs/eval, hash-chained audit, OWASP-ASI evidence. Greenfield or experimental tenants where the gateway investment hasn’t happened.

1 layer pillars 03 · 04 · 07 · 11

03 · Standard AI gateway / VNet

Customer-owned boundary.

Brownfield or regulated estate with existing APIM, NetSec-controlled VNet injection, or Defender for Cloud baseline. Pilot conforms to the established gateway pattern rather than introducing Citadel mid-flight. The safe-check verifies, never prescribes.

How we pick

The SPEC’s § 12 (governance posture) is the source of truth. If the customer hasn’t declared one, threadlight-design asks the question during co-design — explicitly, with the trade-offs — rather than picking silently. If detection finds an existing APIM-Foundry binding, posture 03 wins regardless of what the SPEC says.

Pillars 01 · 03 · 04 · 09

Theme A — network & identity.

The perimeter and the keys. The customer’s NetSec team owns most of this; our job is to surface evidence the agent respects their boundaries, not to invent new ones.

01 · Network posture

Public endpoint vs. private endpoint vs. VNet injection — detected, not declared. We verify the Foundry project’s ingress matches what the SPEC says it should be, and surface a delta if the team shipped a public IP to “just unblock the demo.”

03 · Identity & access

Managed identity, RBAC scope, Entra app registration, group membership. We list every role binding on the Foundry project and flag anything broader than the SPEC’s declared scope (e.g. Owner where Cognitive Services User would do).

04 · Secrets

Key Vault references vs. inline strings. Connection strings, API keys, function keys — all checked against the Vault registry. Inline material is flagged with the exact file:line so the fix is a 30-second edit, not a hunt.

09 · Supply chain

NuGet / pip / npm versions, container base image digest, SBOM presence. Tied to threadlight-deploy’s build manifest, so “what shipped” matches “what we evaluated.” If the manifest is missing, the pillar reports not-verified — never green.

Pillars 02 · 06 · 07 · 08

Theme B — governance & human-in-the-loop.

The agent’s behaviour, evaluated and witnessed. This is where the AI safety conversation lives — not in slides, in evidence.

02 · Agent governance

AGT policy bundle present, signed, and loaded by the runtime. Hash-chained audit log emitting to the customer’s sink. OWASP-ASI evidence pack generated — not as a PDF, as a queryable table.

06 · Continuous evals

Eval suite wired in CI and in the deployed runtime — sampled, not full-firehose. Pass / fail bands per metric, escalation when a band breaks. We surface the last 7 days of eval results next to the readiness scorecard.

07 · Responsible AI

Content filters configured per channel, jailbreak / prompt-injection coverage in the eval suite, refusal patterns tested against the customer’s named edge cases. Linked to threadlight-safe-check --responsible-ai.

08 · HITL & audit

For every high-risk action class declared in the SPEC, a human approval path exists, is enabled, and has been exercised at least once. The audit log shows who approved, when, and on what evidence.

Pillars 05 · 10 · 11

Theme C — operations & cost.

What does it cost to keep running, and how do you know when it breaks? The boring questions that decide whether the pilot makes it to month three.

05 · Observability

Trace context propagation through every hop (Studio → agent → tool → data). Log Analytics workspace wired and emitting. Dashboards seeded so day-one operators don’t have to build them. Alert rules attached to the SLOs declared in the SPEC.

10 · Cost

Hooked into threadlight-consumption-iq — current monthly spend, projected at the SPEC’s declared load, cheaper-SKU recommendations with quality-impact tags. Surfaces the cost delta of a posture change (e.g. “moving to gpt-4.1-mini saves $56/mo, eval delta ≤ 1.2%”).

11 · Reliability

Region pair declared, fail-over runbook exists, throttling policy tested. Retry-with-backoff in the agent harness. We verify the runbook by walking it — not by checking it’s present.

What we don’t do here

We don’t replace the customer’s SRE team or their existing tooling. If they run Datadog, our hooks write OTLP and stop. If they run Azure Monitor end-to-end, we use it natively. The pillar reports posture and conformance, not opinions.

Pillars 12 · 13

Theme D — lifecycle & handover.

The pilot that nobody owns is the pilot that quietly dies. These two pillars are about the boring moment when GBB walks out and the customer’s engineering team walks in.

12 · SRE hand-over

Named on-call. Runbook in their repo, not ours. Alert routing into their paging system. The hand-over pack from threadlight-deploy includes an evidence bundle the customer’s SRE lead can sign off without a call to us.

13 · Model lifecycle

Pinned model version, declared deprecation cadence, rehearsed model-swap procedure. When OpenAI ships a new minor, we have an answer for “do we adopt, and what’s the eval delta?” ready before the customer asks.

How we keep this honest

The pack is generated, not authored. If a hand-over field is empty, it’s empty — we don’t auto-fill plausible text. The customer’s ops lead can tell from the pack alone whether anyone actually walked the runbook.

The graveyard heuristic

If threadlight-production-ready can’t produce a one-page scorecard the customer’s architecture-review board accepts on the spot, the pilot is in the lab graveyard — even if every other pillar is green.

Recap

One scorecard — three colours, no judgement.

Each pillar resolves to green (evidence found, in posture), amber (gap with a named owner and a waiver path), or not-verified (we couldn’t observe, here’s why). Red doesn’t exist on purpose — we never fail a build, we surface a posture.
